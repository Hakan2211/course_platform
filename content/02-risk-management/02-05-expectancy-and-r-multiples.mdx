## THE GENERAL'S MAP TABLE

Picture a war room in 1944. General Eisenhower stands over a massive map table planning the D-Day invasion.
Around him, commanders report on different divisions:

**Commander A:** "Sir, we advanced 12 kilometers today."

**Commander B:** "Sir, we captured 3 villages."

**Commander C:** "Sir, we lost 47 men but eliminated 200 enemy soldiers."

Which commander gave Eisenhower useful information?

Commander C.

Because Commander C reported in relative terms ‚Äî losses versus gains. This ratio tells Eisenhower
whether that division is winning or losing the war.

Commanders A and B reported absolute numbers without context. 12 kilometers sounds impressive ‚Äî
unless you lost 1,000 men to gain it. 3 villages sounds great ‚Äî unless they were abandoned and
tactically worthless.

Professional military commanders don't think in absolute territories or casualties.
They think in ratios, efficiencies, and strategic value.

Professional traders are no different.

If I tell you "I made $5,000 this month," you have no idea if I'm a successful trader or a failing one.

Did I risk $50,000 to make $5,000? (10% return ‚Äî decent)<br/>
Did I risk $500 to make $5,000? (1000% return ‚Äî extraordinary)<br/>
Did I make $5,000 but my account dropped from $50,000 to $45,000?
(Actually lost $45,000, made back $5,000 ‚Äî terrible)
Dollar amounts are meaningless without context.

This is why professional traders invented a new language ‚Äî a measurement system that makes performance comparable, trackable, and meaningful regardless of account size.

This language is built on two concepts:

1\. R-Multiples ‚Äî The universal unit of trading performance
2\. Expectancy ‚Äî The mathematical expression of your edge

## What is an R-Multiple? (THE UNIVERSAL TRADING UNIT)

R (or 1R) = The amount you risked on a trade

That's it. That's the entire definition.

If you risked $100 on a trade, then 1R = $100 for that trade.
If you risked $500 on a trade, then 1R = $500 for that trade.
If you risked $50 on a trade, then 1R = $50 for that trade.

R is your personal, trade-specific unit of risk.

## WHY R-MULTIPLES ARE REVOLUTIONARY

Instead of measuring profit/loss in dollars, we measure in R-multiples:

**Example 1:**

‚Üí You risked $100 (1R)<br/>
‚Üí You made $200 profit<br/>
‚Üí Result: +2R (You made 2√ó what you risked)<br/>

**Example 2:**

‚Üí You risked $100 (1R)<br/>
‚Üí You lost $100<br/>
‚Üí Result: -1R (You lost exactly what you risked)<br/>

**Example 3:**

‚Üí You risked $100 (1R)<br/>
‚Üí You made $350 profit<br/>
‚Üí Result: +3.5R (You made 3.5√ó what you risked)<br/>

**Do you see the elegance?**

R-multiples tell you **how much you made relative to what you risked**,
regardless of account size or dollar amounts.

## THE R-MULTIPLE FORMULA

<Math>R-Multiple = (Profit or Loss) √∑ (Initial Risk)</Math>

Or more precisely:

<Math>
  R-Multiple = (Exit Price - Entry Price) √∑ (Entry Price - Stop Loss Price)
</Math>

For long trades:

<Math>R = (Exit - Entry) √∑ (Entry - Stop)</Math>

For short trades:

<Math>R = (Entry - Exit) √∑ (Stop - Entry)</Math>

### EXAMPLE: CALCULATING R FOR A LONG TRADE

**Trade setup:**

‚Üí Entry: EUR/USD at 1.1000<br/>
‚Üí Stop loss: 1.0950 (50 pips below)<br/>
‚Üí Exit: 1.1150 (150 pips above)<br/>

**Calculate R:**

<Math>
  Initial Risk = 1.1000 - 1.0950 = 50 pips = 1R
  <br />
  Actual Profit = 1.1150 - 1.1000
  <br />= 150 pips R-Multiple = 150 √∑ 50 = 3R
</Math>

**Result: +3R trade**

**Translation:** You made 3 times what you risked. If you risked $100, you made $300.

### EXAMPLE: CALCULATING R FOR A SHORT TRADE

**Trade setup:**

‚Üí Entry: GBP/JPY short at 185.00<br/>
‚Üí Stop loss: 185.60 (60 pips above)<br/>
‚Üí Exit: 184.20 (80 pips below)<br/>

**Calculate R:**

<Math>
  Initial Risk = 185.60 - 185.00 = 60 pips = 1R
  <br />
  Actual Profit = 185.00 - 184.20 = 80 pips
  <br />
  R-Multiple = 80 √∑ 60 = 1.33R
</Math>

**Result: +1.33R trade**

### EXAMPLE: CALCULATING R FOR A LOSING TRADE

**Trade setup:**

‚Üí Entry: Gold at $2,000<br/>
‚Üí Stop loss: $1,990 (risk $10)
‚Üí Stopped out at $1,990

**Calculate R:**

<Math>
  Initial Risk = $10 = 1R
  <br />
  Actual Loss = $10
  <br />
  R-Multiple = -$10 √∑ $10 = -1R
</Math>

**Result: -1R trade**

This is a perfect loss. You lost exactly what you planned to risk, no more, no less.
Your stop did its job.

### EXAMPLE: CALCULATING R FOR A PARTIAL LOSS

**Trade setup:**

‚Üí Entry: AAPL at $180
‚Üí Stop loss: $176 (risk $4)
‚Üí Exited early at $178 (down $2)

**Calculate R:**

<Math>
  Initial Risk = $4 = 1R
  <br />
  Actual Loss = $2
  <br />
  R-Multiple = -$2 √∑ $4 = -0.5R
</Math>

**Result: -0.5R trade**

**Translation:** You lost half of what you were willing to risk.
Maybe you saw deteriorating conditions and exited early ‚Äî smart risk management.

### WHY R-MULTIPLES ARE SUPERIOR TO DOLLAR AMOUNTS

**Consider three traders:**

**TRADER A:**

‚Üí Account: $10,000<br/>
‚Üí Risks 1% per trade = $100<br/>
‚Üí Makes $200 profit

**TRADER B:**

‚Üí Account: $100,000<br/>
‚Üí Risks 1% per trade = $1,000<br/>
‚Üí Makes $2,000 profit

**TRADER C:**

‚Üí Account: $500,000<br/>
‚Üí Risks 1% per trade = $5,000<br/>
‚Üí Makes $10,000 profit

**In dollar terms:**

‚Üí Trader A made $200 (looks weak)<br/>
‚Üí Trader B made $2,000 (looks moderate)<br/>
‚Üí Trader C made $10,000 (looks amazing)

**In R-multiples:**

‚Üí Trader A: $200 √∑ $100 = +2R<br/>
‚Üí Trader B: $2,000 √∑ $1,000 = +2R<br/>
‚Üí Trader C: $10,000 √∑ $5,000 = +2R<br/>

**All three traders had identical performance: +2R.**

**R-multiples make performance comparable across all account sizes.**

This is why professional prop firms, hedge funds, and trading educators measure everything in R.

When you talk to professionals, they don't say "I made $50,000 last month."

They say "I made 28R last month."

And every professional immediately understands what that means:
Exceptional performance, regardless of whether their account is $10,000 or $10 million.

## EXPECTANCY: THE MATHEMATICAL EXPRESSION OF YOUR EDGE

Now we arrive at the single most important metric in trading:

**EXPECTANCY**

Expectancy tells you **how much you expect to make, on average, per trade** over a large sample.

It's the mathematical proof that you have (or don't have) an edge.

### THE EXPECTANCY FORMULA

<Math>
  Expectancy (in R) = (Win Rate √ó Avg Win Size) - (Loss Rate √ó Avg Loss Size)
</Math>

Or more precisely:

<Math>
  Expectancy = (% Wins √ó Avg R of Wins) - (% Losses √ó Avg R of Losses)
</Math>

**Example:**

‚Üí Win rate: 50%<br/>
‚Üí Average win: +2R<br/>
‚Üí Loss rate: 50%<br/>
‚Üí Average loss: -1R<br/>

<Math>
  Expectancy = (0.50 √ó 2R) - (0.50 √ó 1R)
  <br />
  Expectancy = 1.0R - 0.5R
  <br />
  Expectancy = +0.5R per trade
</Math>

**What this means:**

Over 100 trades, you expect to make 100 √ó 0.5R = +50R

If you risk 1% per trade, that's +50% return over 100 trades.

## MAE & MFE: THE ADVANCED METRICS THAT REVEAL HIDDEN TRUTHS

Now we're entering professional territory‚Äîmetrics that 99% of retail traders have never heard of
but that every institutional trader tracks religiously.

### MAE: MAXIMUM ADVERSE EXCURSION

**Definition:** The largest move against you during a trade, before the trade closes.

**Why it matters:** MAE tells you if your stops are too tight or if you're exiting winners too early.

### EXAMPLE: MAE ANALYSIS ON A WINNING TRADE

**Trade:**

‚Üí Entry: 1.1000<br/>
‚Üí Stop: 1.0950 (50 pips, 1R risk)<br/>
‚Üí Exit: 1.1150 (150 pips, +3R win)<br/>

**But during the trade:**

‚Üí Price dropped to 1.0970 before rallying

**MAE calculation:**

<Math>
  MAE = Entry - Lowest Point During Trade
  <br />
  MAE = 1.1000 - 1.0970 = 30 pips
  <br />
  MAE in R = 30 √∑ 50 = 0.6R
</Math>

**What this tells you:**

‚Üí Your stop at 50 pips (1R) was appropriate ‚Äî Price came within 0.6R of your stop but didn't hit it<br/>
‚Üí You gave the trade enough room ‚Äî If your stop was at 0.5R (25 pips),
you would have been stopped out on a +3R winner<br/>
‚Üí Lesson: Don't use stops tighter than 0.6R for this type of setup<br/>

### MAE PATTERN ANALYSIS: THE GOLDMINE

Track MAE across 100 trades. You might discover:

**Pattern 1:** Winners rarely go more than 0.5R against you

‚Üí **Insight:** Your entries are well-timed
‚Üí **Action:** You might be able to use slightly tighter stops (0.7R instead of 1R)

**Pattern 2:** Winners often go 0.8R against you before winning

‚Üí **Insight:** Your entries are early or your stops are too tight
‚Üí **Action:** Either wait for better entries or use 1.2R stops

**Pattern 3:** All your losers went more than 1.2R against you before being stopped

‚Üí **Insight:** You're moving your stops wider when trades go against you
‚Üí **Action:** Stop widening stops! Trust your original stop placement

### MFE: MAXIMUM FAVORABLE EXCURSION

**Definition:** The largest move in your favor during a trade, before the trade closes.

**Why it matters:** MFE tells you if you're letting winners run or cutting them too early.

### EXAMPLE: MFE ANALYSIS ON A LOSING TRADE

**Trade:**

‚Üí Entry: 1.1000<br/>
‚Üí Stop: 1.0950 (50 pips, 1R risk)<br/>
‚Üí Stopped out at 1.0950 (-1R loss)<br/>

**But during the trade:**

‚Üí Price rallied to 1.1080 before reversing

**MFE calculation:**

<Math>
  MFE = Highest Point - Entry
  <br />
  MFE = 1.1080 - 1.1000 = 80 pips
  <br />
  MFE in R = 80 √∑ 50 = 1.6R
</Math>

**What this tells you:**

‚Üí You had a +1.6R profit at one point but ended with -1R loss<br/>
‚Üí You didn't use a trailing stop<br/>
‚Üí You let a winning trade become a losing trade<br/>
‚Üí Lesson: Once you're +1R in profit, trail your stop to at least breakeven<br/>

### MFE PATTERN ANALYSIS: CAPTURING MORE PROFIT

Track MFE across 100 trades. You might discover:

**Pattern 1:** Your winners hit +2.5R MFE but you exit at +1.8R avg

‚Üí **Insight:** You're exiting too early due to fear
‚Üí **Action:** Implement trailing stops, let winners run to +2R minimum

**Pattern 2:** Your losers often show +0.8R MFE before reversing

‚Üí **Insight:** Your thesis was temporarily right, but you didn't protect profits
‚Üí **Action:** Move stop to breakeven once price hits +0.5R to +1R

**Pattern 3:** Your +1R winners often had +3R MFE

‚Üí **Insight:** You're leaving massive profits on the table
‚Üí **Action:** Use wider targets or much looser trailing stops

### MAE/MFE COMBINED ANALYSIS: THE TRUTH MATRIX

**Create a scatter plot of all your trades:**

‚Üí X-axis: MAE (maximum drawdown during trade)<br/>
‚Üí Y-axis: MFE (maximum profit during trade)<br/>
‚Üí Color: Red = Loss, Green = Win

**Patterns you might see:**

**PATTERN A:** "Perfect Execution"

‚Üí Winners: Low MAE (0.3-0.5R), High MFE (2-4R)<br/>
‚Üí Losers: High MAE (0.9-1R), Low MFE (0-0.3R)<br/>
‚Üí Translation: Your stops are perfect, your entries are great, your exits are good

**PATTERN B:** "Stops Too Tight"

‚Üí Winners: Moderate MAE (0.7-0.9R), High MFE (2-4R)<br/>
‚Üí Losers: Low MAE (0.4-0.6R), Moderate MFE (0.5-1R)<br/>
‚Üí Translation: Many losers had winning potential but your stops are too tight

‚úÖ Clean risk-reward (1:2+)
‚úÖ High-conviction setup
‚úÖ Expected win rate: 55-65%

‚ö†Ô∏è Most factors align, 1-2 missing
‚ö†Ô∏è Good structure but against trend, OR with trend but weak structure
‚ö†Ô∏è Decent risk-reward (1:1.5-2)
‚ö†Ô∏è Moderate conviction
‚ö†Ô∏è Expected win rate: 45-55%

**C-GRADE SETUP (0.3-0.5 score):**

‚ö†Ô∏è‚ö†Ô∏è Minimal factors align
‚ö†Ô∏è‚ö†Ô∏è Experimental or learning trade
‚ö†Ô∏è‚ö†Ô∏è Marginal risk-reward (1:1-1.5)
‚ö†Ô∏è‚ö†Ô∏è Low conviction
‚ö†Ô∏è‚ö†Ô∏è Expected win rate: 35-45%
