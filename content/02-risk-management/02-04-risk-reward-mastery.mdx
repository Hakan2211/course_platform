Imagine you're playing chess against a grandmaster. Every move you make, you analyze potential outcomes:

"If I move my knight here, I might lose a pawn but threaten their queen."

"If I sacrifice my rook, I can checkmate in three moves."

"If I play defensively, I might survive but I'll never win."

**Great chess players don't think in absolutes — they think in probabilities and payoffs.**

They calculate: "What's the potential gain versus the potential loss of this move?"

A grandmaster will gladly sacrifice a pawn (small loss) to capture a queen (massive gain).
They'll give up a bishop (medium loss) to force checkmate (ultimate gain).

**They understand asymmetry.**

They understand that winning chess isn't about winning every exchange —
it's about winning the exchanges that matter, where the payoff massively exceeds the risk.

Trading is no different.

In fact, trading is the purest expression of asymmetric thinking in the real world.
You have complete control over your risk (your stop loss).
And you have the opportunity to capture rewards that are multiples of that risk.

This is the secret that separates professional traders from the struggling masses:

You don't need to win most of your trades. You need to win the RIGHT trades —
where your winners are so much bigger than your losers
that you can be wrong 60% of the time and still get rich.

## WHAT IS RISK-REWARD RATIO? (THE DEFINITION THAT CHANGES EVERYTHING)

Let's start with the foundation — what does risk-reward ratio actually mean?

### THE SIMPLE DEFINITION

<Math>Risk-Reward Ratio (RR) = Potential Profit ÷ Potential Loss</Math>
<br />

Or, more precisely:

<Math>RR = (Target Price - Entry Price) ÷ (Entry Price - Stop Loss Price)</Math>

**EXAMPLE 1: The 1:2 Risk-Reward Trade**

**Long EUR/USD:**

→ Entry: 1.1000<br/>
→ Stop loss: 1.0950 (50 pips below)<br/>
→ Target: 1.1100 (100 pips above)

**Calculate RR:**

→ Risk = 50 pips<br/>
→ Reward = 100 pips<br/>
→ RR = 100 ÷ 50 = 2.0

**This is a 1:2 risk-reward ratio.**

For every $1 you risk, you're targeting $2 in profit.

If you risk $100 (your stop loss), you're targeting $200 profit.

Simple, right?

But here's where it gets interesting — and where most traders fail to grasp the profound implications.

## THE PROFITABILITY EQUATION: WIN RATE × RISK-REWARD = YOUR FUTURE

Here's the mathematical truth that will determine whether you succeed or fail as a trader:

**Your profitability is determined by TWO variables:**

1\. Win Rate (% of trades that hit target)<br/>
2\. Risk-Reward Ratio (average reward ÷ average risk)<br/>

You can be profitable with:

→ High win rate + Low RR<br/>
→ Low win rate + High RR<br/>
→ Moderate win rate + Moderate RR<br/>

You CANNOT be profitable with:

→ Low win rate + Low RR<br/>

Let me show you the mathematics that prove this.

### EXAMPLE 1: 1:1 RISK-REWARD

**Breakeven win rate:** 50%

If you use 1:1 RR, you need to win more than half your trades just to break even.

Over 100 trades:

→ 50 wins × 1% = +50%<br/>
→ 50 losses × -1% = -50%<br/>
→ Net: 0% (breakeven)

To profit, you need 51%+ win rate.

This is difficult. Most retail traders can't sustain >50% win rate long-term.

### EXAMPLE 2: 1:2 RISK-REWARD

**Breakeven win rate:** 33.3%

If you use 1:2 RR, you only need to win one-third of your trades to break even.

Over 99 trades:

→ 33 wins × 2% = +66%<br/>
→ 66 losses × -1% = -66%<br/>
→ Net: 0% (breakeven)

To profit, you need 34%+ win rate.

**This is MUCH easier to achieve.**

Even if you're wrong 65% of the time, you can still be profitable.

### EXAMPLE 3: 1:3 RISK-REWARD

**Breakeven win rate:** 25%

If you use 1:3 RR, you only need to win one-quarter of your trades to break even.

You can be wrong 75% of the time and still break even.

To profit, you need 26%+ win rate.

This sounds amazing, right? Just win 30% of trades at 1:3 RR and you're killing it!

But there's a catch (we'll cover this soon).

## THE ASYMMETRIC ADVANTAGE: WHY PROS HUNT HIGH RR

Professional traders are obsessed with risk-reward ratio because it gives them margin for error.

### TRADER A: 1:1 RR, 55% WIN RATE

**Over 100 trades:**

→ Wins: 55 × 1% = +55%<br/>
→ Losses: 45 × -1% = -45%<br/>
→ Net: +10% profit

**Trader A must maintain 55% win rate to stay profitable.**

If their win rate drops to 52%, they barely profit.<br/>
If it drops to 48%, they lose money.

**Razor-thin margin for error.**

### TRADER B: 1:3 RR, 40% WIN RATE

**Over 100 trades:**

→ Wins: 40 × 3% = +120%<br/>
→ Losses: 60 × -1% = -60%<br/>
→ Net: +60% profit

**Trader B is profitable at 40% win rate.**

If their win rate drops to 35%, they're still profitable (+45%).<br/>
If it drops to 30%, they're still profitable (+30%).<br/>
They'd have to drop to 25% win rate to break even.

**Massive margin for error.**

### WHY THIS MATTERS

**Real trading isn't perfect.**

You'll have:

→ Bad days where your focus is off<br/>
→ Bad weeks where the market doesn't cooperate<br/>
→ Bad months where your edge temporarily disappears<br/>
→ Psychological slumps after losing streaks<br/>

**If you operate at 1:1 RR with 52% win rate, a temporary slump to 48% win rate destroys you.**

**If you operate at 1:3 RR with 40% win rate, you can slump to 30% win rate and still be profitable.**

**This is why professionals obsess over risk-reward.**

It's not about being perfect. It's about building a system that's profitable even when you're imperfect.

## THE DIMINISHING RETURNS OF EXTREME RR

Here's the problem with chasing 1:5, 1:10, or higher RR:

Your win rate drops so low that psychological damage becomes severe.

**EXAMPLE: Chasing 1:10 RR**

Breakeven win rate: 9.1%

Sounds great! You only need to win 10% of trades!

**But the reality:**

→ You lose 9 trades in a row (normal at 10% win rate)<br/>
→ Each loss is 1% of your account<br/>
→ You're down 9% before you get a winner<br/>
→ One win brings you to +1% net<br/>

**Psychologically, can you handle nine consecutive losses?**

Most traders can't. They:

→ Start doubting their system after 5 losses<br/>
→ Change their strategy after 7 losses<br/>
→ Give up or revenge trade after 9 losses<br/>

**They never make it to the winning trade.**

**High RR strategies require inhuman psychological discipline.**

## THE OPTIMAL RISK-REWARD: THE PROFESSIONAL SWEET SPOT

Based on decades of professional trading data, here's what actually works:

### THE SWEET SPOT: 1:2 TO 1:3 RR

Why this range is optimal:

→ Achievable win rates (35-50%)<br/>
→ Psychologically sustainable (not too many consecutive losses)<br/>
→ Sufficient asymmetry (winners meaningfully larger than losers)<br/>
→ Works across multiple trading styles<br/>
→ Realistic profit targets (price can actually reach them)<br/>
→ Good margin for error (can drop below 40% win rate and still profit)<br/>

## RR BY TRADING STYLE: WHAT FITS YOUR APPROACH

### SCALPING (Seconds to minutes)

**Optimal RR:** 1:1 to 1:1.5

**Why?**

→ Very short timeframe = smaller price movements<br/>
→ Need high win rate to overcome spreads/commissions<br/>
→ Targets must be realistic (30-50 pips in forex)<br/>
→ Stop losses must be tight (15-25 pips)<br/>

**Typical stats:**

→ Win rate: 55-65%<br/>
→ RR: 1:1 to 1:1.5<br/>
→ Expectancy: 0.1R to 0.3R per trade<br/>

**Challenge:** Death by a thousand cuts if win rate drops

### DAY TRADING (Minutes to hours, no overnight)

**Optimal RR:** 1:1.5 to 1:2

**Why?**

→ Medium timeframe = moderate price movements<br/>
→ Can achieve 1:2 RR within a trading session<br/>
→ Stops can be based on intraday structure<br/>
→ Targets based on support/resistance or ATR<br/>

**Typical stats:**<br/>

→ Win rate: 45-55%<br/>
→ RR: 1:1.5 to 1:2<br/>
→ Expectancy: 0.3R to 0.5R per trade<br/>

**Challenge:** Requires discipline to close all trades by end of day

### SWING TRADING (Days to weeks)

**Optimal RR:** 1:2 to 1:3

**Why?**

→ Longer timeframe = larger price movements possible<br/>
→ Can let trends develop over several days<br/>
→ Stops based on daily/4H structure<br/>
→ Targets based on key levels or measured moves<br/>

**Challenge:** Psychological endurance through multi-day drawdowns

This is where most professional retail traders operate.

### POSITION TRADING (Weeks to months)

**Optimal RR:** 1:3 to 1:5

**Why?**

→ Very long timeframe = massive price movements possible<br/>
→ Catching major trends (500+ pips in forex, 20%+ in stocks)<br/>
→ Wide stops required (200+ pips)<br/>
→ Targets based on major structure or Fibonacci extensions<br/>

**Typical stats:**<br/>

→ Win rate: 30-40%<br/>
→ RR: 1:3 to 1:5<br/>
→ Expectancy: 0.8R to 1.5R per trade<br/>

**Challenge:** Requires extreme patience and psychological fortitude

## HOW TO SET REALISTIC PROFIT TARGETS

Now that you understand optimal RR ratios, let's discuss HOW to actually set your profit targets.

**Rule #1:** Your target must be based on market structure, not arbitrary ratios.

**Wrong approach:**

→ "My stop is 50 pips, so my target is 100 pips (1:2 RR)."<br/>
→ You place target at 100 pips even though there's massive resistance at 80 pips.<br/>
→ Price hits 80 pips, reverses, hits your stop.<br/>
→ You turned a winning trade into a loser because you ignored structure.

**Right approach:**

→ "My stop is 50 pips. The next major resistance is at 120 pips.
That's 1:2.4 RR. I'll target 115 pips to stay below resistance."<br/>
→ Price hits your target at 115 pips before reaching resistance.<br/>
→ Winner.<br/>

**Your RR ratio is determined by market structure, not the other way around.**

### METHOD #1: SUPPORT/RESISTANCE TARGETS

**For longs:**

→ Target = Next major resistance level

**For shorts:**

→ Target = Next major support level

**EXAMPLE:**

**Long EUR/USD at 1.1000**<br/>

→ Stop: 1.0950 (50 pips below, at support breakdown)<br/>
→ Next resistance: 1.1120 (120 pips above)<br/>
→ Target: 1.1115 (120 pips, just below resistance)<br/>
→ RR: 120 ÷ 50 = 2.4

### METHOD #2: ATR-BASED TARGETS

Use ATR to set targets that adapt to volatility (same principle as ATR stops).

**Formula:**

<Math>Target = Entry ± (ATR × Multiplier)</Math>

**Common multipliers:**

→ 2× ATR: Conservative target (higher win rate)<br/>
→ 3× ATR: Balanced target (moderate win rate)<br/>
→ 4× ATR: Aggressive target (lower win rate)<br/>

**EXAMPLE:**

**Long EUR/USD at 1.1000**<br/>

→ 14-period ATR: 60 pips<br/>
→ Stop: 3× ATR = 180 pips below = 1.0820<br/>
→ Target: 3× ATR = 180 pips above = 1.1180<br/>
→ RR: 180 ÷ 180 = 1:1<br/>
→ Wait—that's only 1:1 RR!

**Solution:** Use asymmetric ATR multipliers:

→ Stop: 3× ATR = 180 pips below<br/>
→ Target: 6× ATR = 360 pips above = 1.1360<br/>
→ RR: 360 ÷ 180 = 2:1

### METHOD #3: MEASURED MOVES

For chart patterns, use the pattern's height to project targets.

**EXAMPLE:** Ascending Triangle

**Pattern height:** 100 pips (from support to resistance)

**Breakout:** 1.1000
**Stop:** Below triangle support at 1.0950 (50 pips)
**Target:** Breakout + Pattern height = 1.1000 + 100 = 1.1100

**RR:** 100 ÷ 50 = 2:1

### METHOD #4: FIBONACCI EXTENSIONS

For trend trades, use Fibonacci extensions (1.272, 1.618, 2.618) as targets.

**EXAMPLE:**

**Uptrend with pullback:**

→ Swing low: 1.0900<br/>
→ Swing high: 1.1100 (200 pips rally)<br/>
→ Pullback to: 1.1000 (50% retracement)

**Entry:** 1.1000 (pullback bounce)
**Stop:** 1.0950 (below pullback low)
**Target:** 1.618 Fibonacci extension = 1.1224

**RR:** (1.1224 - 1.1000) ÷ (1.1000 - 1.0950) = 224 ÷ 50 = 4.48:1

### METHOD #5: PARTIAL TARGETS (SCALING OUT)

Professional approach: Take partial profits at multiple levels.

**EXAMPLE:**

**Long EUR/USD at 1.1000, stop at 1.0950**

**Profit targets:**

→ Target 1: 1.1050 (50 pips, 1:1 RR) → Close 33% of position
→ Target 2: 1.1100 (100 pips, 1:2 RR) → Close 33% of position
→ Target 3: 1.1200 (200 pips, 1:4 RR) → Close final 33%

**Why this works:**

→ Lock in profits early (Target 1 hit = you're guaranteed breakeven or better)<br/>
→ Let runners capture big moves (Target 3 captures exceptional moves)<br/>
→ Psychological benefit (seeing profits locked in reduces anxiety)<br/>
→ Improves overall RR (even if only T1 and T2 hit, you average 1:1.5 RR)<br/>

**This is how professionals trade.**

**Scaling Example:**

Instead of one target, use 2-3 targets at different levels.

**Example:**

→ Position: 300 shares<br/>
→ Entry: $100<br/>
→ Stop: $95 (risk = $5 per share)

**Targets:**

→ Target 1: $107.50 (1.5:1) → Exit 100 shares (1/3 position)<br/>
→ Target 2: $112.50 (2.5:1) → Exit 100 shares (1/3 position)<br/>
→ Target 3: $120+ (4:1+) → Exit 100 shares (1/3 position) or trail stop<br/>

**The Mathematics of Scaling**

**If all targets hit:**

→ First 100 shares: +$7.50 each = $750<br/>
→ Second 100 shares: +$12.50 each = $1,250<br/>
→ Third 100 shares: +$20 each = $2,000<br/>
→ Total profit: $4,000<br/>
→ Total risk: 300 shares × $5 = $1,500<br/>
→ Effective RRR: 2.67:1

**If only first target hits:**

→ 100 shares at +$7.50 = $750<br/>
→ 200 shares stopped at -$5 = -$1,000<br/>
→ Net: -$250<br/>
→ You still lost, but less than if you had held full position

**The Advantage:**

→ Takes profits off the table (guarantees some wins)<br/>
→ Lets runners capture extended moves<br/>
→ Reduces psychological pressure<br/>
→ Optimizes for both high win rate and high RRR

**The Disadvantage:**

→ More complex to manage<br/>
→ Increased commission costs<br/>
→ May exit too early on huge moves<br/>
→ Partial fills can complicate execution

## Slippage, Costs, and Real-World Execution

Your theoretical RRR isn't your actual RRR once you account for market realities.

### Slippage: The Hidden Tax

Slippage is the difference between your intended price and your actual execution price.

**Example:**

Planned trade:

→ Entry: $100<br/>
→ Stop: $95<br/>
→ Target: $110<br/>
→ Theoretical RRR: 2:1

**Actual execution:**

→ Entry: $100.05 (slippage on entry)<br/>
→ Stop: $94.85 (slippage on stop)<br/>
→ Target: $109.90 (slippage on exit)<br/>

**Actual trade:**

→ Risk: $100.05 - $94.85 = $5.20<br/>
→ Reward: $109.90 - $100.05 = $9.85<br/>
→ Actual RRR: 1.89:1<br/>

Your 2:1 trade just became a 1.89:1 trade due to slippage.

### Slippage by Market Type

**Highly Liquid (SPY, major forex):**

→ Slippage: 0.01-0.05%<br/>
→ Minimal impact on RRR

**Moderately Liquid (Most large-cap stocks):**

→ Slippage: 0.05-0.15%<br/>
→ Moderate impact on RRR

**Low Liquidity (Small-caps, options):**

→ Slippage: 0.2-1%+<br/>
→ Significant impact on RRR

**Highly Illiquid (Penny stocks, exotic pairs):**

→ Slippage: 1-5%+<br/>
→ Devastating impact on RRR

### Commission and Spread Impact

**Example: Forex Trade**

→ Spread: 2 pips ($20 on standard lot)<br/>
→ Risk: $100<br/>
→ Reward: $300<br/>
→ Theoretical RRR: 3:1

**After spread:**

→ Real risk: $100 + $20 = $120<br/>
→ Real reward: $300 - $20 = $280<br/>
→ Real RRR: 2.33:1

The spread cost you 22% of your asymmetry.

### The Professional's Execution Edge

**1\. Use Limit Orders When Possible**<br/>
Control your entry price rather than accepting market price.

**2\. Avoid Market Orders in Illiquid Assets**<br/>
You'll give away significant edge to slippage.

**3\. Account for Costs in Your Calculations**<br/>
If you need 3:1 RRR after costs, target 3.5:1 before costs.

**4\. Test Execution Quality**<br/>
Track your actual fills vs intended prices. Measure your real slippage.

**5\. Adjust Position Size in Illiquid Markets**<br/>
Your smaller size will get better fills and preserve RRR.

## Order Execution Risk: The Overlooked Danger

Beyond spreads and slippage, there are catastrophic execution risks you must understand:

### RISK #1: GAP RISK (Weekend/Overnight)

**The scenario:**

→ You're long EUR/USD at 1.1000<br/>
→ Stop at 1.0950<br/>
→ Risk: 50 pips = $100<br/>

**Friday close:** EUR/USD at 1.1020 (you're up 20 pips)

**Weekend event:** Major geopolitical crisis

**Monday open:** EUR/USD gaps down to 1.0850 (150 pips below your stop)

**Your stop is at 1.0950, but price never traded there.**

**Execution:**

→ Your stop triggers at the first available price: 1.0850<br/>
→ Actual loss: 150 pips = $300 (3× your planned risk)

**Your 1% risk trade became a 3% loss.**

**This is gap risk. It's real. It happens.**

### RISK #2: FLASH CRASH / LIQUIDITY VACUUM

**The scenario:**

→ You're long GBP/USD at 1.3000<br/>
→ Stop at 1.2950<br/>
→ Risk: 50 pips

**Sudden news:** Unexpected central bank announcement

**Market reaction:**

→ GBP/USD plummets 200 pips in 10 seconds<br/>
→ Liquidity evaporates (no buyers)<br/>
→ Your stop at 1.2950 executes at 1.2820 (first available price)

**Actual loss:** 180 pips (3.6× your planned risk)

**This happened during the 2016 GBP flash crash, the 2019 AUD flash crash, and countless other events.**

### RISK #3: BROKER STOP LOSS FAILURE

**The scenario:**

→ Your broker's platform crashes during high volatility<br/>
→ Your stop loss order is in the system but doesn't execute<br/>
→ By the time the platform recovers, you're down 5× your planned risk

**Or worse:**

→ Your broker goes bankrupt (yes, this happens)<br/>
→ Your stop orders are not honored<br/>
→ You're left with massive losses

### HOW TO MITIGATE EXECUTION RISK

→ Never hold positions through weekends if you can't afford gap risk<br/>
→ Reduce position size before major news events<br/>
→ Use reputable, well-capitalized brokers<br/>
→ Don't trade during extreme low-liquidity periods<br/>
→ Consider guaranteed stop-loss orders (higher cost but guaranteed execution)<br/>
→ Understand that your 1% risk can become 2-3% in black swan events<br/>
→ This is why portfolio heat limits matter (Lesson 2.7)

## THINKING IN PROBABILITIES: THE PROFESSIONAL MINDSET

Everything we've covered so far is preparation for this critical mindset shift:

**Amateurs think in certainties. Professionals think in probabilities.**

### THE AMATEUR MINDSET

→ "This trade will definitely work—it's a perfect setup!"<br/>
→ "I need to win this trade to make back my losses."<br/>
→ "If I lose this one, I'm a terrible trader."<br/>
→ "This trade can't lose—all my indicators agree."<br/>
→ Thinks each trade is unique and critical<br/>
→ Emotionally attached to outcomes<br/>
→ Focuses on being "right"<br/>

### THE PROFESSIONAL MINDSET

→ "This trade has a 45% probability of hitting my target. That's enough edge with my 1:2 RR."<br/>
→ "This is trade #43 in my 100-trade sample. Individual outcome is irrelevant."<br/>
→ "Even my best setups lose 40% of the time. That's expected."<br/>
→ "My edge manifests over 100+ trades, not in any single trade."<br/>
→ Thinks in statistical distributions<br/>
→ Emotionally detached from outcomes<br/>
→ Focuses on process and probabilities<br/>

### THE POKER ANALOGY

Imagine a professional poker player with pocket aces (the best starting hand).

→ Probability of winning: ~85%<br/>
→ Probability of losing: ~15%<br/>

They bet aggressively because the odds favor them.

But they lose 15% of the time — and that's OKAY.

When they lose with pocket aces, they don't think:

→ "I'm a terrible poker player!"<br/>
→ "This game is rigged!"<br/>
→ "I should never play aces again!"

They think:

→ "I played the probabilities correctly."<br/>
→ "This outcome was within expected variance."<br/>
→ "Over 100 hands with aces, I'll win ~85 and lose ~15."<br/>
→ "The process was correct, even though this outcome was negative."<br/>

**Trading is identical.**

Your 1:2 RR setup with 50% win rate will lose 50% of the time.

**That's not a failure. That's statistics.**

### THE 100-TRADE PERSPECTIVE

Professional traders think in samples of 100+ trades.

**TRADER A (Amateur thinking):**

→ Takes Trade #1: Loses<br/>
→ "Ugh, this system doesn't work."<br/>
→ Takes Trade #2: Loses<br/>
→ "I knew it. I need a new strategy."<br/>
→ Abandons system after 5 trades

TRADER B (Professional thinking):

→ Takes Trade #1: Loses<br/>
→ "Expected. 1 of 100."<br/>
→ Takes Trade #2: Loses<br/>
→ "Expected. 2 of 100."<br/>
→ Takes Trades #3-10: 6 losses, 2 wins<br/>
→ "8 losses, 2 wins so far. Need 98 more trades for statistical significance."<br/>
→ After 100 trades: 48 losses, 52 wins at 1:2 RR<br/>
→ Net result: (52 × 2R) - (48 × 1R) = +56R = Highly profitable

**Trader A never made it to the profitable outcome.**<br/>
**Trader B executed with discipline and let their edge manifest.**

## THE RISK-REWARD EXECUTION CHECKLIST

Before every trade, run through this protocol:

### PRE-TRADE RR PROTOCOL

1\. Identify your trade thesis (why you're entering)<br/>
2\. Place your stop based on invalidation point (technical or ATR)<br/>
3\. Identify next major resistance (long) or support (short)<br/>
4\. Set your target just before that level<br/>
5\. Calculate your RR ratio (Target - Entry) ÷ (Entry - Stop)<br/>
6\. Verify RR is at least 1:1.5 (minimum acceptable)<br/>
7\. If RR &lt;1:1.5, don't take the trade (insufficient asymmetry)<br/>
8\. Adjust position size based on your RR (higher RR = potentially larger size within risk limits)<br/>
9\. Consider scaling out at multiple targets (1:1, 1:2, 1:3)<br/>
10\. Account for spread/commission in your calculation (5-10% erosion)<br/>
11\. Confirm this trade fits your style's optimal RR (scalp: 1:1-1.5, swing: 1:2-3)<br/>
12\. Accept that this trade has X% chance of losing (expected, not personal)<br/>

### DURING-TRADE RR PROTOCOL

1\. Do NOT move your target closer (let your RR play out)<br/>
2\. Do NOT exit early due to fear (trust your RR plan)<br/>
3\. DO consider trailing stop once 1:1 hit (protect profits)<br/>
4\. Monitor price action at your target level (resistance might cause rejection)<br/>
5\. If using scaling out, execute at your predetermined levels<br/>
6\. Do NOT move your target further away hoping for more (greed destroys RR discipline)

### POST-TRADE RR ANALYSIS

1\. Record actual RR achieved (not just planned RR)<br/>
2\. Note execution costs (spread, slippage, commission)<br/>
3\. Compare planned vs. actual target (did market structure validate your target?)<br/>
4\. Assess: Was my RR realistic for this setup? (learn and adjust)<br/>
5\. Track your average RR over 20+ trades (see your true RR distribution)<br/>
6\. Calculate your expectancy (more on this in Lesson 2.5)

### The Risk-Reward Decision Tree

Use this framework for every setup evaluation:

**Step 1: Identify Setup**<br/>
Chart pattern, support/resistance, catalyst, etc.

**Step 2: Determine Stop Placement**<br/>
Where is thesis invalidated? Where is technical stop?<br/>

**Step 3: Identify Target**<br/>
Next resistance, measured move, Fibonacci extension?<br/>

**Step 4: Calculate RRR**<br/>
Target distance / Stop distance = ?<br/>

**Step 5: Apply Decision Rules**<br/>
RRR < 1.5:1 → Skip trade
Not enough asymmetry. Pass.

RRR 1.5:1 to 2:1 → Marginal
**Only take if:**

→ Very high conviction<br/>
→ High win rate setup<br/>
→ Scalping/day trading where this is normal<br/>

**RRR 2:1 to 3:1 → Standard**<br/>
Solid trade if setup quality is good.

**RRR 3:1 to 5:1 → Excellent**
Strong asymmetry. Take if thesis is sound.

**RRR 5:1+ → Exceptional**<br/>
Rare opportunity. Verify target is realistic.

**Step 6: Adjust for Setup Quality**

**A-grade setup:**<br/>
Minimum RRR required: 2:1

**B-grade setup:**<br/>
Minimum RRR required: 2.5:1

**C-grade setup:**<br/>
Minimum RRR required: 3:1<br/>
Lower quality setups need more asymmetry to be worth the risk.
