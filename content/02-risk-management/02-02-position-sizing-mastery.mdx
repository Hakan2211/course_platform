## The Position Sizing Hierarchy

Before we dive into specific methods, you need to understand the hierarchy of position sizing decisions.
Think of this as building a fortress layer by layer:

### Layer 1: Account Risk Tolerance (The Bedrock)

**Question:** How much of my total account am I willing to risk on a single trade?

This is your starting point. Everything else flows from this decision.

**Professional standard:** 1-2% per trade
**Conservative:** 0.5-1% per trade
**Aggressive (dangerous):** 3-5% per trade
**Suicidal:** 5%+ per trade

### Layer 2: Setup Quality Assessment (The Foundation Stones)

**Question:** Does this specific setup deserve my full risk allocation, or less?

Not all setups are created equal. A perfect A+ setup might get your full 2%.
A questionable C-grade setup might only get 0.5%.

### Layer 3: Market Volatility Adjustment (The Adaptive Layer)

**Question:** How volatile is this market right now, and how should that affect my size?

In calm markets, you can trade normal size. In highly volatile markets, you reduce size to account
for the increased uncertainty.

### Layer 4: Technical Stop Placement (The Execution Layer)

**Question:** Where does my stop-loss need to be based on market structure?

Your stop placement is determined by technical analysis—support/resistance, chart patterns, volatility.
Your position size is then calculated backward from your stop distance.

### Layer 5: Leverage and Margin Considerations (The Reality Check)

**Question:** Can I actually execute this position size given my broker's margin requirements?

Finally, you verify that your intended position size is executable within your margin constraints.

**This is the correct sequence.** Most amateur traders do it backward—they pick a position size
based on "what feels right," then slap on a random stop-loss. That's building your fortress in quicksand.

## THE FOUNDATION: FIXED FRACTIONAL POSITION SIZING

Let's start with the bedrock — the non-negotiable baseline that every trader must master:

**Fixed Fractional Position Sizing**

This is beautifully simple:

**You risk the same percentage of your current capital on every trade.**

Not the same dollar amount. Not a random amount based on how you feel. **The same percentage.**

### The Formula

<Math>
  Position Size = (Account Size × Risk %) / (Entry Price - Stop Loss Price)
</Math>
<br />

Or more universally:

<Math>Position Size = (Account Risk in $) / (Trade Risk in $)</Math>
<br />

Let's break this down with a concrete example:

### EXAMPLE 1: FOREX TRADE (EUR/USD)

**Your situation:**

→ Account size: $10,000<br/>
→ Risk per trade: 1% = $100<br/>
→ Entry price: 1.1000<br/>
→ Stop loss: 1.0950<br/>
→ Trade risk: 50 pips (0.0050)<br/>

**For forex, position size is calculated in lots:**<br/>

<Math>Position Size = Risk in $ / (Pips at Risk × Pip Value)</Math>

For EUR/USD, 1 pip = $10 per standard lot (100,000 units)

<Math>
  Position Size = $100 / (50 pips × $10 per lot)
  <br />
  Position Size = $100 / $500 per lot
  <br />
  Position Size = 0.2 standard lots = 2 mini lots = 20 micro lots
</Math>

**Translation:** You'd trade 20,000 units of EUR/USD

If your stop is hit, you lose exactly $100 (1% of your account).
If you hit a 1:2 target at 1.1100 (100 pips), you make $200 (2% of your account).

**This is the baseline. This is your shield.**

### EXAMPLE 2: STOCK TRADE (AAPL)

**Your situation:**

→ Account size: $50,000<br/>
→ Risk per trade: 1% = $500<br/>
→ Entry price: $180.00<br/>
→ Stop loss: $176.00<br/>
→ Trade risk: $4.00 per share<br/>

<Math>Position Size = $500 / $4.00 Position Size = 125 shares</Math>

**Translation:** You'd buy 125 shares of AAPL

If your stop is hit, you lose exactly $500 (1% of your account).
If you hit a 1:2 target at $188.00 (+$8), you make $1,000 (2% of your account).

### EXAMPLE 3: CRYPTO TRADE (BTC/USD)

**Your situation:**

→ Account size: $20,000<br/>
→ Risk per trade: 1.5% = $300<br/>
→ Entry price: $45,000<br/>
→ Stop loss: $44,000<br/>
→ Trade risk: $1,000 per BTC<br/>

<Math>Position Size = $300 / $1,000 Position Size = 0.3 BTC</Math>

**Translation:** You'd buy 0.3 Bitcoin

If your stop is hit, you lose exactly $300 (1.5% of your account).
If you hit a 1:2 target at $47,000 (+$2,000 per BTC), you make $600 (3% of your account).

## ADVANCED METHOD #1: ATR-BASED VOLATILITY SIZING

Fixed fractional sizing (1-2% risk) is your baseline.
But professional traders add a crucial layer of sophistication:

**Adjusting position size based on market volatility.**

Why? Because not all markets and not all trades have the same volatility characteristics.

Consider:

→ Low volatility: EUR/USD during London session (average 50 pip range)<br/>
→ High volatility: GBP/JPY during news event (average 200 pip range)<br/>

If you place your stops at the same technical levels without adjusting for volatility, you'll either:

1\. Get stopped out prematurely in high volatility (noise takes you out)<br/>
2\. Risk too much in low volatility (tight stop means huge position)

**ATR (Average True Range) solves this.**

### WHAT IS ATR?

ATR measures the average range of price movement over a specified period (typically 14 periods).

→ High ATR = High volatility = Need wider stops = Smaller position size<br/>
→ Low ATR = Low volatility = Can use tighter stops = Larger position size

### THE ATR POSITION SIZING FORMULA

<Math>
  Stop Distance = Entry Price ± (ATR × Multiplier) Position Size = (Account Risk
  in $) / Stop Distance in $
</Math>

**Typical ATR multipliers:**

→ 2× ATR: Aggressive (more likely to get stopped out by noise)<br/>
→ 3× ATR: Balanced (professional standard)<br/>
→ 4× ATR: Conservative (gives trade more room)<br/>

### EXAMPLE: ATR-BASED SIZING ON EUR/USD

**Your situation:**

→ Account: $10,000<br/>
→ Risk per trade: 1% = $100<br/>
→ Entry: 1.1000<br/>
→ 14-period ATR: 0.0060 (60 pips)<br/>
→ Multiplier: 3× ATR<br/>

**Calculate stop distance:**

<Math>
  Stop Distance = 3 × 60 pips = 180 pips Stop Loss = 1.1000 - 0.0180 = 1.0820
</Math>

**Calculate position size:**

<Math>
  Position Size = $100 / (180 pips × $10 per lot) Position Size = $100 / $1,800
  per lot Position Size = 0.0555 lots ≈ 5.5 micro lots (5,550 units)
</Math>

**Compare to fixed 50-pip stop:**

<Math>
  Position Size = $100 / (50 pips × $10 per lot) Position Size = 0.2 lots
  (20,000 units)
</Math>

**Notice the difference?**

ATR-based sizing gave you a **3.6× smaller position** because the wider stop
(appropriate for volatility) automatically reduced your exposure.

**This is beautiful risk management:**

→ You're still risking exactly 1% ($100)<br/>
→ But your stop is intelligently placed based on market volatility<br/>
→ You won't get stopped out by normal market noise<br/>
→ Your position size automatically adjusts to maintain consistent risk

### THE ATR ADVANTAGE IN DIFFERENT MARKET CONDITIONS

**Scenario 1: Low Volatility (ATR = 30 pips)**

<Math>
  Stop = 3 × 30 = 90 pips Position Size = $100 / (90 × $10) = 0.111 lots (11,100
  units)
</Math>

**Scenario 2: High Volatility (ATR = 120 pips)**

<Math>
  Stop = 3 × 120 = 360 pips Position Size = $100 / (360 × $10) = 0.0278 lots
  (2,780 units)
</Math>

**See the elegance?**

In low volatility, you get a larger position because you can use a tighter stop.<br/>
In high volatility, you get a smaller position because you need a wider stop.

**But in both cases, you're risking exactly 1% of your account.**

Your position size adapts to market conditions automatically. This is professional-grade risk management.

## ADVANCED METHOD #2: THE KELLY CRITERION (USE WITH CAUTION)

Now we enter dangerous territory.

The **Kelly Criterion** is a mathematical formula developed by John Kelly Jr. in 1956 to determine
optimal bet sizing for favorable odds.

Many professional traders use it. Many more have been destroyed by it.

**The Kelly formula:**

<Math>Kelly % = (Win Rate × Avg Win) - (Loss Rate × Avg Loss) / Avg Win</Math>

Or simplified for trading:

<Math>Kelly % = Win Rate - [(1 - Win Rate) / Risk-Reward Ratio]</Math>

### EXAMPLE: KELLY CALCULATION

**Your system:**

→ Win rate: 55%<br/>
→ Risk-reward ratio: 1:2<br/>
→ Average win: $200<br/>
→ Average loss: $100<br/>

<Math>
  Kelly % = 0.55 - [(1 - 0.55) / 2] Kelly % = 0.55 - 0.225 Kelly % = 0.325 =
  32.5%
</Math>

**The Kelly Criterion says you should risk 32.5% of your capital per trade.**

**DO NOT DO THIS.**

### WHY FULL KELLY IS SUICIDE

Full Kelly maximizes long-term growth rate, but it comes with catastrophic drawdown risk:

→ Full Kelly (32.5%): Max drawdown can exceed 80%<br/>
→ One bad streak and you're psychologically obliterated<br/>
→ Requires perfect estimation of win rate and RR (impossible in trading)<br/>
→ Assumes infinite number of trials (you don't have infinite capital)<br/>

**Professional traders use fractional Kelly:**

→ Half Kelly (16.25%): Still too aggressive; max drawdown ~50%<br/>
→ Quarter Kelly (8.1%): Aggressive but survivable; max drawdown ~25%<br/>
→ One-Eighth Kelly (4%): Conservative; max drawdown ~15%<br/>

**Most professionals use 1/4 to 1/8 Kelly at most.**

### PRACTICAL KELLY APPLICATION

Let's redo the calculation with Quarter Kelly:

<Math>
  Full Kelly = 32.5%
  <br />
  Quarter Kelly = 32.5% / 4 = 8.1%
  <br />
</Math>

**Still too high for most traders.**

**Here's the intelligent approach:**

Use Kelly as an **upper bound**, not a target:

1\. Calculate Full Kelly
2\. Divide by 4 to 8
3\. Cap at 2% regardless

**Example:**

→ Full Kelly = 32.5%<br/>
→ Divide by 8 = 4.06%<br/>
→ Cap at 2% = Use 2%<br/>

**You're now using Kelly to confirm that your 1-2% risk is within optimal parameters,
not to encourage reckless sizing.**

### KELLY WARNING SIGNS

If your Kelly calculation suggests >50% position sizing:

→ Your win rate estimate is probably wrong<br/>
→ Your risk-reward estimate is probably wrong<br/>
→ You're overfitting to a small sample size<br/>
→ **Do NOT increase position size**<br/>

If your Kelly calculation suggests &lt;1% position sizing:

→ Your system might not have a genuine edge
→ Your risk-reward ratio might be too poor
→ You might need to refine your strategy

**Bottom line: Kelly is a mathematical curiosity and a reality check,
not a position sizing method for most traders.**

## ADVANCED METHOD #3: RISK SCORING (THE PROFESSIONAL EDGE)

Not all trade setups are created equal.

Your A+ setups—perfect confluences, ideal conditions, maximum edge—deserve more capital allocation
than your B or C setups.

**Risk Scoring allows you to dynamically adjust position size based on setup quality.**

### THE RISK SCORING FRAMEWORK

**A-Grade Setup (Score: 1.0):**

→ Multiple confirmations<br/>
→ Strong trend alignment<br/>
→ Key support/resistance<br/>
→ High probability zone<br/>
→ Optimal market conditions<br/>
→ **Allocation: Full risk (1-2%)**

**B-Grade Setup (Score: 0.5-0.75):**

→ Moderate confirmation<br/>
→ Acceptable but not perfect conditions<br/>
→ One or two factors missing<br/>
→ **Allocation: Half to 3/4 risk (0.5-1.5%)**

**C-Grade Setup (Score: 0.25-0.5):**

→ Minimal confirmation<br/>
→ Counter-trend or choppy conditions<br/>
→ Experimental or learning trade<br/>
→ **Allocation: Quarter to half risk (0.25-1%)**

**D-Grade Setup (Score: 0):**

→ Poor setup<br/>
→ **Allocation: Do not trade**

### EXAMPLE: RISK SCORING IN ACTION

**Your situation:**

→ Account: $10,000<br/>
→ Base risk: 1% = $100<br/>

**Trade 1: A-Grade Setup**

→ Score: 1.0<br/>
→ Risk: $100 × 1.0 = $100 (full risk)<br/>

**Trade 2: B-Grade Setup**

→ Score: 0.6<br/>
→ Risk: $100 × 0.6 = $60 (reduced risk)<br/>

**Trade 3: C-Grade Setup**

→ Score: 0.3<br/>
→ Risk: $100 × 0.3 = $30 (minimal risk)<br/>

**The beauty of this approach:**

→ You're still never exceeding your 1% base risk<br/>
→ You're allocating more capital to your best opportunities<br/>
→ You're reducing exposure to marginal setups<br/>
→ You're training yourself to be selective<br/>
→ Your overall risk-adjusted returns improve<br/>

## LEVERAGE, EXPOSURE, AND MARGIN: THE HIDDEN MULTIPLIERS

Now let's address the silent killer that most traders don't fully understand:

**Leverage and margin mechanics.**

### WHAT IS LEVERAGE?

Leverage allows you to control a position larger than your account balance.

**Examples:**

→ 10:1 leverage: $1,000 controls $10,000 position<br/>
→ 50:1 leverage: $1,000 controls $50,000 position<br/>
→ 100:1 leverage: $1,000 controls $100,000 position<br/>

**Critical distinction:**

**Available leverage ≠ Used leverage**

Your broker might offer 10:1 leverage, but that doesn't mean you should use it.

Used leverage is determined by your position size relative to your account:

<Math>Used Leverage = Total Position Value / Account Equity</Math>

### EXAMPLE: CONTROLLING YOUR ACTUAL LEVERAGE

**Your situation:**

→ Account: $10,000<br/>
→ Broker offers: 100:1 leverage<br/>
→ You trade: 0.1 standard lots of EUR/USD (10,000 units)<br/>
→ Position value: $11,000 (if EUR/USD = 1.1000)<br/>
→ Your used leverage:

<Math>Used Leverage = $11,000 / $10,000 = 1.1:1</Math>

Even though your broker offers 100:1, you're only using 1.1:1.

**This is intelligent leverage use.**

### THE LEVERAGE DANGER ZONE

**Safe Zone (1:1 to 5:1 used leverage):**

→ Position value is 1-5× your account<br/>
→ Allows for normal market movements<br/>
→ Low risk of margin calls<br/>
→ **Recommended for most traders**<br/>

**Caution Zone (5:1 to 10:1 used leverage):**

→ Position value is 5-10× your account<br/>
→ Requires tight stop losses<br/>
→ Moderate risk of margin calls<br/>
→ **Only for experienced traders with robust systems**<br/>

**Danger Zone (10:1 to 30:1 used leverage):**

→ Position value is 10-30× your account<br/>
→ Very high risk of margin calls<br/>
→ Small adverse moves can wipe account<br/>
→ **Only for professionals with exceptional risk management**<br/>

**Suicide Zone (30:1+ used leverage):**

→ Position value exceeds 30× your account<br/>
→ Almost certain destruction<br/>
→ **Avoid at all costs**<br/>

### MARGIN CALL MATHEMATICS

Your broker requires **margin** — a percentage of your position value held as collateral.

<Math>Margin Requirement = Position Value / Leverage</Math>

**Example:**

→ Position: $50,000<br/>
→ Leverage: 50:1<br/>
→ Margin required: $50,000 / 50 = $1,000<br/>

**Margin call trigger:**

Most brokers issue margin calls when your equity falls to **100-50%** of required margin.

**If you use high leverage, a small adverse move triggers margin call:**

→ Account: $2,000<br/>
→ Position: $100,000 (50:1 leverage)<br/>
→ Margin required: $2,000<br/>
→ Available for losses: ~$0-$1,000<br/>

**A 1% adverse move = $1,000 loss = margin call.**

**Compare to low leverage:**

→ Account: $10,000<br/>
→ Position: $20,000 (2:1 leverage)<br/>
→ Margin required: $400 (at 50:1 available)<br/>
→ Available for losses: ~$9,600<br/>

**A 1% adverse move = $200 loss = barely noticeable.**

**The lesson: Control your actual position size, not just your percentage risk.**

## PORTFOLIO HEAT: THE FORGOTTEN RISK

Position sizing isn't just about individual trades — it's about total exposure.

**Portfolio heat = Total capital at risk across all open positions**

### THE PORTFOLIO HEAT RULE

**Never have more than 6-10% of your total capital at risk simultaneously.**

**Example:**

You have a $20,000 account and follow 1% risk per trade:

**Maximum simultaneous positions:**

→ 8% max portfolio heat<br/>
→ Risk per trade: 1% = $200<br/>
→ Maximum positions: 8 trades<br/>

**If you have 10 positions open, each risking $200:**

→ Total portfolio heat: $2,000 = **10% of account**<br/>
→ You've hit your limit — **do not take another trade**<br/>

### WHY PORTFOLIO HEAT MATTERS

Scenario: The Correlation Trap

You think you're diversified:

→ Long EUR/USD<br/>
→ Long GBP/USD<br/>
→ Long AUD/USD<br/>
→ Long NZD/USD<br/>
→ Long EUR/GBP<br/>

**Reality: All five are USD shorts.**

If USD strengthens, **all five positions lose simultaneously.**

What you thought was 5× 1% risk (5% total) is actually more like **1 position with 5% risk**.

**During the 2016 Trump election surprise, USD spiked. Traders with multiple USD shorts
got obliterated in minutes.**

### MANAGING PORTFOLIO HEAT

→ Track total risk daily<br/>
→ Limit correlated positions (no more than 2-3 in same direction)<br/>
→ Reduce position size when portfolio heat >6%<br/>
→ Close or reduce positions before adding new ones<br/>
→ Use a position tracker (Excel, TradingView, journal)<br/>

**Professional approach:**

If you want to add a 9th position but you're at 8% portfolio heat:

→ Option A: Reduce size on all positions to 0.9% each (allows 9 positions at 8.1% heat)<br/>
→ Option B: Close or reduce your weakest position<br/>
→ Option C: Don't take the new trade<br/>

**Discipline over opportunity.**

## SCALING FOR DIFFERENT ACCOUNT SIZES

Position sizing isn't one-size-fits-all. Your approach must scale with your capital.

### SMALL ACCOUNTS ($500-$5,000)

**Challenges:**

→ Minimum position sizes (1 micro lot = $0.10/pip)<br/>
→ Commission/spread eats larger % of profits<br/>
→ Leverage required to make meaningful gains<br/>

**Strategy:**

→ Use 1% risk maximum<br/>
→ Trade micro lots only initially<br/>
→ Focus on high-probability setups (A-grade only)<br/>
→ Prioritize capital preservation and learning<br/>
→ Consider fractional positions (0.3 micro lots if broker allows)<br/>
→ Accept that growth will be slow but steady<br/>
→ Do NOT overtrade to "grow faster"<br/>

**Example: $1,000 account**

→ 1% risk = $10 per trade<br/>
→ With 50-pip stop = 0.2 micro lots (200 units)<br/>
→ Even perfect trade (+100 pips) = $20 profit<br/>

**It's slow. That's the point. You're building skill and discipline.**

### MEDIUM ACCOUNTS ($5,000-$50,000)

**Advantages:**

→ More flexibility in position sizing<br/>
→ Can trade mini lots<br/>
→ Commissions less impactful<br/>

**Strategy:**

→ Use 1-1.5% risk per trade<br/>
→ Implement ATR-based sizing<br/>
→ Apply risk scoring (A/B/C grades)<br/>
→ Maintain portfolio heat limits<br/>
→ Begin tracking expectancy and R-multiples<br/>
→ Scale up gradually as consistency proven<br/>

**Example: $20,000 account**

→ 1% risk = $200 per trade<br/>
→ With 100-pip stop on EUR/USD = 0.2 standard lots (20,000 units)<br/>
→ Perfect trade (+200 pips) = $400 profit<br/>

**You're now trading with professional parameters.**

### LARGE ACCOUNTS ($50,000+)

**Advantages:**

→ Full position sizing flexibility<br/>
→ Can diversify across multiple markets<br/>
→ Commission/spread minimal impact<br/>

**Strategy:**

→ Use 0.5-1% risk per trade (smaller % as account grows)<br/>
→ Full ATR and volatility-based sizing<br/>
→ Sophisticated risk scoring systems<br/>
→ Multiple uncorrelated positions<br/>
→ Consider hedging strategies<br/>
→ Implement professional risk software<br/>
→ Focus on risk-adjusted returns, not just returns<br/>

**Example: $100,000 account**

→ 0.5% risk = $500 per trade<br/>
→ With 75-pip stop on GBP/JPY = 0.05 standard lots (5,000 units)<br/>
→ Perfect trade (+150 pips) = $750 profit<br/>

**At this level, preservation matters more than growth.**

### The Psychological Bridge

When your account grows from $50,000 to $100,000, don't immediately double your position sizes. Instead:

**Week 1-4:** Keep risking the same dollar amounts ($1,000 per trade)<br/>
**Week 5-8:** Slowly increase to $1,250 per trade<br/>
**Week 9-12:** Move to $1,500 per trade<br/>
**Month 4+:** Gradually scale to full 2% of new balance ($2,000)<br/>

This gives your psychology time to adjust to larger dollar swings.

## THE SCALING PARADOX

**As your account grows, your percentage risk should DECREASE, but your absolute dollar profits increase.**

**Small account (aggressive growth phase):**

→ $5,000 × 1.5% = $75 risk per trade<br/>
→ Need high % returns to grow meaningfully<br/>

**Large account (preservation phase):**

→ $200,000 × 0.5% = $1,000 risk per trade<br/>
→ Lower % returns generate substantial income<br/>

**This is how professionals think:**

They don't risk more as they get richer. They risk **less in percentage terms** but more in absolute terms,
and they **compound consistently**.

## BUILDING YOUR POSITION SIZE CALCULATOR

Every trader needs a reliable position sizing calculator. Here's how to build one:

### BASIC EXCEL CALCULATOR

**Inputs:**

→ Account Size<br/>
→ Risk %<br/>
→ Entry Price<br/>
→ Stop Loss Price<br/>
→ Instrument (for pip/point value)<br/>

**Formula:**

<Math>
  Risk in $ = Account Size × Risk % Trade Risk = ABS(Entry Price - Stop Loss
  Price) Position Size = Risk in $ / Trade Risk (in units or $)
</Math>

**For forex:**

<Math>
  Position Size (lots) = Risk in $ / (Pips at Risk × Pip Value per Lot)
</Math>

### ADVANCED CALCULATOR WITH AMATEUR

**Additional inputs:**

→ ATR value<br/>
→ ATR multiplier<br/>

**Formula:**

<Math>
  Stop Distance = ATR × Multiplier Stop Loss = Entry ± Stop Distance Position
  Size = Risk in $ / Stop Distance in $
</Math>

### PROFESSIONAL CALCULATOR WITH RISK SCORING

**Additional inputs:**

→ Setup Grade (A/B/C)<br/>
→ Score multiplier (1.0 / 0.6 / 0.3)<br/>

**Formula:**

<Math>
  Adjusted Risk = Base Risk % × Score Multiplier Position Size = (Account Size ×
  Adjusted Risk) / Trade Risk
</Math>

## THE POSITION SIZING CHECKLIST

Before EVERY trade, run through this checklist:

### PRE-TRADE POSITION SIZING PROTOCOL

1.  Calculate current account balance<br/>
2.  Determine risk % for this trade (1-2% baseline)<br/>
3.  Identify exact entry and stop loss prices<br/>
4.  Calculate trade risk in pips/points/$<br/>
5.  Check ATR and adjust stop if using volatility sizing<br/>
6.  Grade setup quality (A/B/C) and adjust risk if using scoring<br/>
7.  Calculate exact position size<br/>
8.  Check total portfolio heat (add this trade's risk)<br/>
9.  Verify portfolio heat &lt;8-10%<br/>
10. Confirm margin requirements and leverage<br/>
11. Double-check all calculations<br/>
12. Place trade with exact position size and stop loss<br/>

If you skip any step, you're gambling, not trading.

## COMMON POSITION SIZING MISTAKES (AND HOW TO AVOID THEM)

### MISTAKE #1: Sizing based on desired profit

**Wrong:** "I want to make $500, so I'll calculate position size backward from my target."

**Right:** "I'll risk 1% ($100), place my stop at the technical level, and let the position size fall where it may."

**Your desired profit is irrelevant. Your risk management is paramount.**

### MISTAKE #2: Using the same position size for all trades

**Wrong:** "I always trade 0.5 lots regardless of stop distance."

**Right:** "My position size adjusts based on my stop loss distance to maintain consistent risk percentage."

**Fixed lot size = variable risk = eventual destruction.**

### MISTAKE #3: Increasing size after wins, decreasing after losses

**Wrong:** "I just won three trades, I'm on fire—let's risk 5% on this one!"

**Right:** "My position size is determined by my system, not my recent results."

**This is the fastest way to give back all profits and more.**

### MISTAKE #4: Risking more on "sure things"

**Wrong:** "This setup is perfect — I'm risking 5% because it can't lose."

**Right:** "No trade is certain. I risk my predetermined percentage regardless of confidence."

**The trades you're most confident in are often the ones that hurt the most.**

### MISTAKE #5: Ignoring portfolio heat

**Wrong:** "I have five positions each risking 2%, that's fine — it's only 2% per trade."

**Right:** "I have 10% portfolio heat, which is my limit. I cannot take another position until one closes."

**Simultaneous losses destroy accounts.**

### MISTAKE #6: Not adjusting for account growth

**Wrong:** "My account doubled, but I still risk the same dollar amount."

**Right:** "My risk percentage stays constant, which means my dollar risk grows with my account."

**Compounding requires adjusting absolute risk as your account grows.**

## THE PSYCHOLOGICAL POWER OF PROPER POSITION SIZING

Beyond the mathematics, proper position sizing has profound psychological benefits:

### BENEFIT #1: REMOVES FEAR

When you risk 1%, losing doesn't hurt. You can take your stop without hesitation.

When you risk 10%, every tick against you is torture. You'll move your stop or hold hoping for a reversal.

**Small position size = clear thinking.**

### BENEFIT #2: ENABLES DISCIPLINE

With proper sizing, you can follow your system mechanically without emotional interference.

With oversizing, every decision becomes emotional—should I hold? Should I exit early? Should I add to this loser?

**Proper sizing = robotic execution.**

### BENEFIT #3: BUILDS CONFIDENCE

A 10-trade losing streak at 1% risk is annoying but manageable.

A 3-trade losing streak at 10% risk is psychologically devastating.

**Surviving losses builds confidence. Proper sizing makes survival guaranteed.**

### BENEFIT #4: ALLOWS PATIENCE

When your positions are sized properly, you can let winners run without anxiety.

When oversized, every move against you feels catastrophic, causing premature exits.

**Small risk = big patience.**

## KEY TAKEAWAYS

→ Position sizing is where 90% of profitability is determined—more important than entries or exits<br/>
→ Fixed fractional sizing: Risk the same percentage (1-2%) on every trade, not the same dollar amount<br/>
→ The position size formula: (Account Risk in $) / (Trade Risk in $) = Position Size<br/>
→ Never risk more than 1-2% per trade—this allows you to survive 20+ consecutive losses<br/>
→ Recovery mathematics are cruel: A 50% loss requires a 100% gain to break even<br/>
→ ATR-based sizing adjusts position size for volatility—wider stops in volatile markets = smaller positions<br/>
→ Kelly Criterion at full size is suicide—use fractional Kelly (1/4 to 1/8) as an upper bound, capped at 2%<br/>
→ Risk scoring allows dynamic allocation: A-grade setups get full risk, B/C grades get reduced risk<br/>
→ Leverage is a weapon, not a tool—control your actual used leverage, not just available leverage<br/>
→ Portfolio heat (total risk across all positions) must never exceed 6-10% of account<br/>
→ Position sizing must scale with account size—larger accounts use smaller % risk but larger $ risk<br/>
→ Proper position sizing provides psychological benefits: removes fear, enables discipline, builds confidence<br/>
