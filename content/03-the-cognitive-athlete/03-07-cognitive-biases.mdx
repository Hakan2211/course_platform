## The Trader Who Couldn't See What Was Right in Front of Him

His name was Daniel. PhD in Economics. Former risk analyst at Goldman Sachs. Literally taught courses on behavioral finance at a top university.

He could lecture for hours about cognitive biases. He knew every study, every principle, every theoretical framework. He'd explain to his students how irrational people are, how predictably they fall into thinking traps, how emotions cloud judgment.

Then he'd close his laptop, open his trading platform, and make every single mistake he'd just spent two hours teaching others to avoid.

February 2023. Daniel was long a tech stock at $87. His thesis was solid: Earnings beat expected, guidance raised, technical breakout confirmed. His stop was at $84.50. His target was $94.

The trade immediately moved in his favor. $88... $89... $90...

At $91, the stock paused. Normal consolidation. But Daniel felt uneasy. He moved his stop up to $89 (lock in profit).

The stock dipped to $89.20, then bounced. He felt relief but also frustration—"I almost got stopped out."

At $92, he moved his stop again to $91.50.

The stock pulled back to $91.80. His heart raced. "This keeps almost stopping me out. Maybe I should just take profit here."

At $92.50, he exited. "I'll take the $5.50 gain. Clean winner."

The stock ran to $96 over the next two hours.

He sat there, watching it, feeling that unique agony of being right about the direction but still losing money by exiting early.

Then he did something he immediately regretted: He re-entered at $95.80.

"It's still going. My original target was $94, and we're already above that. This could go to $100."

The stock hit $96.20, then reversed hard. News broke that a similar company had given weak guidance.

$95... $94... $93...

Daniel held. "It'll come back. My original entry was $87, so even at $93, I'd still be up. This is just noise."

$92... $91... $90...

"Okay, it's back to where I took profit the first time. But I know this stock. The fundamentals haven't changed. I'm not selling at a loss when I was just up."

$89... $88... $87...

"This is exactly where I originally entered! It's like I never made any profit at all. But if I sell now, I'm admitting I was wrong. And I WASN'T wrong—the stock did go to $96, I just managed it poorly."

$86... $85... $84.50 (his original stop)...

"The original stop was too tight anyway. I need to give it room."

$83... $82...

At $81, Daniel finally capitulated. Sold everything in disgust.

Final tally:

First trade: +$5.50/share profit (exited too early)
Second trade: -$14.80/share loss (held too long)
Net result: -$9.30/share
If he had just followed his original plan—entry $87, stop $84.50, target $94—he would have exited at $94 for a +$7 gain.

Instead, he managed to lose money on a trade that went 10% in his favor.

<hr />

That evening, Daniel sat down to journal his trade. And as he wrote, he started recognizing the pattern.

Everything he'd taught his students about cognitive biases had played out in his own trading:

Loss Aversion: He couldn't stand the thought of that $5.50 profit turning into a loss, so he took it early. Then he couldn't stand the thought of realizing the loss on his re-entry, so he held it into a disaster.

Anchoring: His entry price of $87 became an emotional anchor. When the stock returned to $87, his brain screamed "I'm back to breakeven!" even though that was irrelevant to whether the trade was still valid.

Confirmation Bias: After he re-entered, he only looked for information that supported holding. He ignored the similar-company news. He dismissed the technical breakdown. He cherry-picked fundamentals that supported his thesis.

Recency Bias: Because the stock had just run to $96, his brain overweighted that recent move and assumed it would continue. He gave more weight to the last two hours than to the entire context.

He had fallen into every trap. Despite knowing better. Despite teaching others about these exact mistakes.

Daniel wrote in his journal that night: "I am not immune to my own psychology. Knowledge is not protection. I don't need to learn more about biases. I need systems that prevent me from acting on them."

<hr />

This lesson is about the cognitive biases that destroy trading accounts—not in theory, but in practice.

You already know you shouldn't hold losers and cut winners.
You already know you shouldn't let your entry price dictate your decisions.
You already know you should look at contradictory evidence.
You already know you shouldn't chase.

And you do it anyway.

Because these aren't knowledge problems. They're architecture problems—built-in flaws in how your brain processes information.

No amount of intelligence protects you. Daniel had a PhD in economics and still couldn't escape his own biases.

Because biases aren't about intelligence. They're about the fundamental shortcuts your brain uses to process the overwhelming amount of information in markets.

You can't eliminate biases. But you can build systems to prevent them from trading your account.

## BIAS #1: LOSS AVERSION - Why Losses Hurt More Than Wins Feel Good

### The Asymmetry

In 1979, psychologists Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky published a paper that would eventually win a Nobel Prize.

They asked people a simple question:

Scenario A: You definitely gain $100.
Scenario B: You have a 50% chance to gain $200, 50% chance to gain $0.

Which do you choose?

Most people chose Scenario A (the sure thing), even though both scenarios have the same expected value ($100).

Then they asked the opposite:

Scenario C: You definitely lose $100.
Scenario D: You have a 50% chance to lose $200, 50% chance to lose $0.

Most people chose Scenario D (the gamble), even though the expected value is the same (-$100).

What this revealed:

People are risk-averse when facing gains (they want certainty).
People are risk-seeking when facing losses (they'll gamble to avoid the loss).

But the key finding was the asymmetry:

Through extensive testing, Kahneman and Tversky discovered that losses hurt approximately 2 to 2.5 times more than equivalent gains feel good.

Losing $100 creates about 2-2.5x the emotional intensity as winning $100.

This isn't a personality trait. It's not cultural. It's not learned.

It's universal human psychology, likely hardwired by evolution.

### Why Evolution Made Us Loss-Averse

On the ancestral savanna, the costs of losses and gains were not symmetrical.

Gaining extra food: Nice to have. Increases survival probability slightly.

Losing access to food: Potentially fatal. Could mean starvation.

Gaining status: Helpful. Might improve mating opportunities.

Losing status: Could mean expulsion from tribe = death.

In survival terms, losses threatened death. Gains provided marginal improvements.

Natural selection favored individuals who were highly motivated to avoid losses, even at the cost of missing potential gains.

The overly cautious survived. The risk-neutral did not.

Your DNA reflects the survival strategies of your ancestors.

And in their world, loss aversion was adaptive.

But in trading, loss aversion is catastrophic.

### How Loss Aversion Destroys Trading

Manifestation #1: Holding Losers Too Long
You enter a trade. It goes against you. Your stop is at $84.50. The stock hits $84.60.

Your brain: "If I close this now, I've definitely locked in a loss. But if I hold, there's still a chance it comes back. Maybe the stop is too tight. Maybe it'll bounce from here."

What's happening:

Loss aversion makes you risk-seeking in the face of losses. You'll gamble (hold the loser) to avoid the pain of realizing the loss.

The irony: Holding the loser often turns a small loss into a large loss. You avoid the pain of $200 by creating the pain of $2,000.

Manifestation #2: Cutting Winners Too Early
You enter a trade. It goes in your favor. You're up $300. Your target is $800.

Your brain: "I could lock in this $300 for sure. But if I hold and it reverses, I might lose this profit. A bird in the hand is worth two in the bush."

What's happening:

Loss aversion makes you risk-averse in the face of gains. You'll take the certain smaller profit to avoid the pain of losing paper gains.

The irony: You're treating unrealized profit like it's already yours. The pain of "losing" unrealized profit feels like a real loss.

Manifestation #3: The Certainty Effect
Kahneman and Tversky also discovered that people overweight certainty.

A 100% chance of $100 feels much better than it should compared to a 95% chance of $105.

Certainty has disproportionate emotional appeal.

Trading impact:

You see that +$500 profit and the "close position" button offers certainty. Even though your system says hold, your brain craves that certainty.

You click.

Meanwhile, that -$200 loss doesn't offer certainty—it could still come back! So you hold, seeking the uncertain hope of recovery.

You systematically take certain small gains and hold uncertain large losses.

This is the recipe for account destruction.

### The Math of Loss Aversion

Let's say losses hurt 2.5x more than gains feel good.

Your system has:

60% win rate
Average win: $500
Average loss: $300
Expected value per trade: (0.60 × $500) - (0.40 × $300) = +$180
But your emotional experience:

Win: +$500 × 1.0 = +500 emotional units
Loss: -$300 × 2.5 = -750 emotional units

Even though you're profitable mathematically, you're in emotional pain overall.

After 10 trades (6 wins, 4 losses):

Financial result: ($500 × 6) - ($300 × 4) = $3,000 - $1,200 = +$1,800 profit
Emotional result: (500 × 6) - (750 × 4) = 3,000 - 3,000 = 0 emotional profit (or negative)
You're making money but it doesn't feel like it.

This emotional deficit drives traders to:

Change perfectly good systems
Increase position size to get bigger wins
Hold losers to avoid the pain
Overtrade to "make up for" the emotional hole
All of which destroys the edge that was actually working.

### The Loss Aversion Protocol

Recognition:
Ask yourself:

Am I holding this position because my system says hold, or because I don't want to realize the loss?
Am I exiting this winner because my system says exit, or because I want to lock in the certain gain?
Would I enter this trade right now at the current price? (If no, why are you still in it?)
Intervention:
Strategy 1: Pre-Commitment

Set hard stops and targets BEFORE entry. Don't give yourself the opportunity to decide in the moment.

Use bracket orders if your platform supports them. Automate the execution.

Why this works: You're making the decision when your loss aversion isn't activated (before you have a position). Once you're in the trade, loss aversion is active and will corrupt your judgment.

Strategy 2: The "New Trade" Test

When you're in a losing position and considering holding, ask: "If I had no position, would I enter at this price right now based on my system?"

If no, exit. You're only holding because of loss aversion.

Strategy 3: Mental Accounting Trick

The moment you enter a trade, consider that capital spent.

Don't think: "I'm up $300" or "I'm down $200."

Think: "I invested $5,000 in this position. It's currently worth $5,300" or "...currently worth $4,800."

This reduces the loss aversion by reframing. You're not "losing money"—you're managing an investment with fluctuating value.

Strategy 4: Scale Your Emotional Response

Deliberately practice feeling the same about $100 gain as $100 loss.

When you win: Don't celebrate excessively. Acknowledge it neutrally.
When you lose: Don't catastrophize. Acknowledge it neutrally.

This is the most difficult intervention, but the most powerful.

Elite traders have trained emotional symmetry—wins and losses register with similar emotional intensity.

They've manually corrected the 2.5x asymmetry down to something closer to 1:1.

## BIAS #2: CONFIRMATION BIAS - Seeing What You Want to See

### The Evidence Filter

Confirmation bias is the tendency to seek, interpret, and remember information that confirms your existing beliefs while ignoring or dismissing contradictory information.

It's not that you're stupid. It's that your brain is trying to be efficient.

Your brain operates on predictions. It builds a model of the world, then looks for evidence that confirms the model is correct.

When you find confirming evidence: "Good, my model is accurate. Move on."
When you find contradictory evidence: "Probably noise. My model is still mostly right."

This works fine in stable environments.

It's catastrophic in markets.

### How Confirmation Bias Destroys Trading

Manifestation #1: Seeing Only Bull or Bear Evidence
You're long a stock. Your thesis: "This company is undervalued and will re-rate higher."

Then negative news comes out: Competitor gaining market share. Revenue growth slowing. Key executive leaving.

Your brain: "That's already priced in. / That's short-term noise. / They're restructuring, this is good long-term. / The market is overreacting."

Why? Because you're long. Your brain needs the position to be right, so it filters reality to support that need.

Meanwhile, every piece of positive news—no matter how trivial—feels incredibly significant.

"They released a new product feature!" → Bullish
"CEO bought 100 shares!" → Bullish
"Analyst maintained their price target!" → Bullish

You're not evaluating evidence objectively. You're building a case for what you already believe.

Manifestation #2: Selective Chart Reading
You see a potential setup. Your bias: This is going up.

You look at the chart and see:

Higher lows (bullish!) ✓
Volume increasing (bullish!) ✓
Above the 50-day MA (bullish!) ✓
You don't see (or minimize):

Lower highs (bearish, but you call it "consolidation")
Momentum divergence (bearish, but you don't check)
Resistance just above (bearish, but "it'll break through")
You're not analyzing the chart. You're shopping for evidence to support the trade you already want to take.

Manifestation #3: Social Media Echo Chamber
You have a position. You go to Twitter/StockTwits/Discord.

You unconsciously seek out and engage with:

Posts that agree with your position
People who are also long/short
Analysis that confirms your thesis
You unconsciously avoid or dismiss:

Posts with opposing views
People with opposite positions
Analysis that contradicts your thesis
Algorithms make this worse—social platforms show you more of what you engage with.

You've created a custom-built confirmation machine.

Manifestation #4: Post-Trade Rationalization
After a losing trade, confirmation bias shifts to protect your ego:

"The setup was actually good, I just got unlucky."
"The market was irrational today."
"This is just temporary, it'll prove me right eventually."
"My analysis was correct, my timing was just off."

You're selectively remembering the parts of your analysis that were right and forgetting the parts that were wrong.

This prevents learning. If you can't accurately assess what went wrong, you can't improve.

Part 8: The Confirmation Bias Protocol
Strategy 1: The Devil's Advocate Protocol
Before entering a trade, force yourself to articulate the bear case (if you're going long) or bull case (if you're going short).

Write it down:

"Reasons this trade could fail:"

"Evidence that contradicts my thesis:"

If you can't think of any, you're already compromised by confirmation bias. Don't take the trade.

Strategy 2: Pre-Mortem Analysis
Imagine it's one week from now. The trade has failed spectacularly.

Write down: "This trade failed because..."

This forces you to think about failure scenarios before you're emotionally invested.

Strategy 3: Seek Contradictory Evidence
Actively look for reasons your trade is wrong:

Read analysis from people with opposite views
Look for technical evidence that contradicts your setup
Find fundamental data that challenges your thesis
If you can't find any, you're not looking hard enough.

Every trade has risk. Every thesis has flaws. If you can't see them, your confirmation bias is active.

Strategy 4: Outside Review
Show your trade idea to someone who has no position and no bias.

Ask them: "What am I not seeing? What could go wrong?"

External perspective breaks the confirmation bubble.

Strategy 5: Post-Trade Objective Review
After closing a trade (win or loss), review it objectively:

"What was I right about?"
"What was I wrong about?"
"What did I see?"
"What did I miss?"
"What evidence did I dismiss that I shouldn't have?"

Do this for BOTH wins and losses.

You can have confirmation bias in winning trades too—you might have won despite being wrong about key factors.

## BIAS #3: ANCHORING & THE DISPOSITION EFFECT

### The Irrelevant Number That Controls Your Decisions

Anchoring is the cognitive bias where you rely too heavily on the first piece of information you receive (the "anchor") when making decisions.

In trading, the anchor is almost always your entry price.

And it's completely, utterly, totally irrelevant to what the market will do next.

But it doesn't feel irrelevant. It feels like the most important number in the world.

### How Anchoring Destroys Trading

The Disposition Effect:
Researchers Hersh Shefrin and Meir Statman discovered a pattern: Investors systematically hold losing investments too long and sell winning investments too soon.

They called this the Disposition Effect—the disposition to sell winners and hold losers.

The cause? Anchoring to your entry price.

Example:
You buy at $50.

Scenario A: Stock goes to $55.

Your brain: "I'm up $5! That's 10% profit! That's good, I should lock it in."

The anchor ($50) makes $55 feel like success. You sell.

Scenario B: Stock goes to $45.

Your brain: "I'm down $5. That's a 10% loss. But if I just wait for it to get back to $50, I'll be fine. I'm not selling at a loss."

The anchor ($50) makes $45 feel like failure that needs to be corrected. You hold.

The Problem:

The market doesn't care that you bought at $50.

Whether the stock at $45 is a good investment has nothing to do with whether you paid $50 for it.

Whether the stock at $55 should be held has nothing to do with whether you paid $50 for it.

But your brain can't separate your entry price from your decision-making.

Manifestation #1: Holding to "Get Back to Breakeven"
This is the most common and destructive form of anchoring.

"I'm not selling until I at least get back to breakeven."

Why? What's magical about breakeven?

Answer: Nothing. Except that's your anchor.

You're making a position-sizing and risk-management decision based on an emotionally significant but economically irrelevant number.

Manifestation #2: "House Money" Effect
You're up $1,000 on a trade. Now that $1,000 feels like "house money"—not really yours.

So you get more reckless with it. You hold positions longer. You take bigger risks.

"I'm playing with the market's money now."

No, you're not. It's your money. It's in your account.

But the anchor (your original capital) makes the profit feel different, less real, less valuable.

Manifestation #3: The Sunk Cost Fallacy
You're down $2,000 on a trade.

"I've already lost so much, I might as well hold and see if it recovers. I can't take this loss now after coming this far."

This is sunk cost fallacy—making decisions based on money already spent rather than on future expected value.

The $2,000 is gone. The only relevant question is: "Is this position worth holding based on what I know now?"

But the anchor of your accumulated loss makes you hold positions you shouldn't.

### The Mental Accounting Trap

Richard Thaler won a Nobel Prize partly for his work on mental accounting—the tendency to treat money differently depending on where it came from or what account it's in.

Trading examples:

"That money came from profits, so I can risk more with it."
"That money is from my savings, so I have to be more careful with it."
"I already made $500 today, so I can afford to risk $300 on this marginal setup."

Reality: Money is fungible. A dollar from profits spends exactly the same as a dollar from savings.

But anchors and mental categories make us treat identical dollars as different.

### The Anchoring Protocol

Strategy 1: Hide Your Entry Price
Many platforms let you hide your P&L or average entry price.

Use this feature.

If you can't see your entry, it's harder to anchor to it.

Strategy 2: The "New Trader" Test
When deciding whether to hold or exit, ask:

"If I had no position, would I enter right now at the current price?"

If yes: Hold.
If no: Exit.

This removes the anchor. You're evaluating the trade as if you're starting fresh.

Strategy 3: Think in Percentages, Not Dollars
Instead of: "I'm down $2,000, I need to get back to breakeven."

Think: "This position has declined 15%. Based on my system, is this still a valid hold?"

Percentages are less emotionally charged than absolute dollars and reduce anchoring.

Strategy 4: Position Diary Entry
When you enter a trade, write down:

"I will exit this trade if: ******\_\_\_******"

Define your exit criteria BEFORE you have losses or gains to anchor to.

Then, during the trade, you're not deciding based on your P&L—you're executing your pre-defined exit criteria.

Strategy 5: Breakeven Is Not Special
Deliberately practice closing trades at breakeven.

Your brain needs to learn that breakeven is not psychologically significant.

It's just a price. Sometimes you exit above it. Sometimes below it. Sometimes right at it.

None of these is more significant than the others.

## BIAS #4: RECENCY BIAS & HERD BEHAVIOR

### The Last Thing You Saw Is the Most Important

Recency bias is the tendency to overweight recent events and underweight historical data.

Your brain assumes recent patterns will continue.

This was adaptive in stable environments—if it rained yesterday and today, it will probably rain tomorrow.

But markets aren't stable environments.

Recent moves are often exactly the wrong data to extrapolate from.

### How Recency Bias Destroys Trading

Manifestation #1: The Hot Hand Fallacy
You've won the last 4 trades in a row.

Your brain: "I'm hot right now. I'm seeing the market clearly. I should trade bigger and more often."

Reality: You probably caught favorable variance. Your edge hasn't changed. Your skill hasn't improved.

But recent wins loom much larger in your mind than your overall historical performance.

Result: You overtrade and oversize exactly when variance is most likely to turn against you.

Manifestation #2: The Loser's Freeze
You've lost the last 3 trades in a row.

Your brain: "I can't pick a trade right now. Everything I touch loses. I should sit out."

Reality: You probably hit unfavorable variance. Your edge hasn't disappeared. Your skill hasn't declined.

But recent losses loom much larger than your overall historical performance.

Result: You miss good setups exactly when variance is likely to turn in your favor.

Manifestation #3: Chasing
A stock has gone straight up for the last 3 hours.

Your brain: "This is the momentum! This is the move! I need to get in!"

Reality: You're seeing a move that's already happened and extrapolating it into the future.

Result: You enter near the top, right before the reversal.

Manifestation #4: "The Market Has Changed"
Your system stops working for two weeks.

Your brain: "The market has changed. My edge is gone. I need a new system."

Reality: You're in a normal drawdown that happens periodically to all systems.

But recent performance (two weeks) overwhelms historical performance (two years of profitability).

Result: You abandon a working system at exactly the wrong time.

### Herd Behavior - The Social Dimension

Humans are tribal. Your brain has mirror neurons that literally simulate what others around you are doing.

When everyone is buying, your brain screams: "BUY!"
When everyone is selling, your brain screams: "SELL!"

This isn't conscious. It's neurological.

The Neuroscience:
When you observe someone taking an action, your mirror neurons fire as if you're taking that action.

This creates an unconscious bias toward imitation.

In tribal survival, this was adaptive: If everyone is running, you should run too. Don't wait to see the tiger—just run with the tribe.

In markets, this is catastrophic: By the time "everyone" is buying, you're late. By the time "everyone" is selling, it's often the bottom.

FOMO (Fear of Missing Out):
You see others making money on a trade you didn't take.

Your brain: "Everyone is getting in on this! I'm being left behind! I need to join!"

Neurochemically:

Your dopamine system activates (seeing others get rewards)
Your social pain centers activate (being excluded from the group)
Your fear system activates (missing the opportunity)
This triple activation creates overwhelming urge to join the trade.

By the time you feel FOMO, you're almost certainly too late.

### The Recency & Herd Protocol

Strategy 1: Statistical Context
Keep a spreadsheet of your last 100 trades.

When you have a winning or losing streak, look at it in context:

"I've had 4 wins in a row. In my last 100 trades, I've had six streaks of 4+ wins. This is normal variance, not a hot hand."

"I've had 3 losses in a row. In my last 100 trades, I've had eight streaks of 3+ losses. This is normal variance, not broken skill."

Context defeats recency.

Strategy 2: Time-Weighted Review
When reviewing performance, don't just look at recent trades.

Deliberately review trades from 3 months ago, 6 months ago, 1 year ago.

This forces your brain to incorporate older data and reduces recency weighting.

Strategy 3: The "Why Now?" Test
When you feel the urge to enter a trade you didn't previously plan to take, ask:

"Why am I taking this now? What new information have I received?"

If the answer is: "Because it's been going up" or "Because everyone is talking about it"—that's recency and herd bias.

Don't take the trade.

Strategy 4: Mute the Herd
During your trading hours, mute social media.

No Twitter. No StockTwits. No Discord. No CNBC.

The herd is loudest at extremes—tops and bottoms.

You don't need their input. You need your system.

Check social media after your trading session is done, not during.

Strategy 5: Invert Recent Performance
This is advanced, but powerful:

When you're on a winning streak, tighten your risk management (smaller sizes, stricter criteria).

When you're on a losing streak (but your system is sound), maintain your risk management or slightly loosen your criteria.

This is the opposite of what your brain wants to do.

But it's statistically correct. Winning streaks often precede reversals. Losing streaks often precede recoveries.

## The Bias Dashboard

Create a pre-trade checklist that forces you to check for all four biases:

Before entering any trade, answer:

☐ Loss Aversion Check: Am I taking this trade because it's a good setup, or because I want to make back recent losses?

☐ Confirmation Bias Check: Have I looked for evidence that contradicts this trade? What's the bear case (if long) or bull case (if short)?

☐ Anchoring Check: Am I considering this trade based on its merits right now, or because of where I entered/exited a previous position?

☐ Recency Check: Am I taking this because my system says to, or because of recent price action or recent performance (mine or the market's)?

If you can't answer all four cleanly, don't take the trade.

## Key Takeaways:

✓ Loss aversion makes losses hurt 2-2.5x more than equivalent gains feel good—this is universal human psychology, not a personal flaw

✓ You systematically hold losers and cut winners—because your brain is risk-seeking in losses and risk-averse in gains

✓ Confirmation bias makes you see only supporting evidence—you unconsciously filter reality to match your position

✓ The Devil's Advocate protocol forces contradictory thinking—actively seeking disconfirming evidence breaks the confirmation bubble

✓ Your entry price is an irrelevant anchor—but it controls your decisions unless you actively remove it

✓ The "New Trader" test defeats anchoring—ask "Would I enter now?" to evaluate positions without the entry price bias

✓ Recency bias makes you overweight the last few trades—winning streaks create overconfidence, losing streaks create paralysis

✓ Statistical context defeats recency—tracking 100+ trades shows you that streaks are normal variance

✓ Herd behavior is neurological, not psychological—mirror neurons make you want to imitate others automatically

✓ Biases can't be eliminated, only managed—you need systems and protocols that prevent biases from executing trades

## The Truth:

These aren't character flaws. They're evolutionary survival mechanisms misfiring in financial markets.

Loss aversion kept your ancestors from taking fatal risks.
Confirmation bias allowed quick decisions with incomplete information.
Anchoring provided reference points in a complex world.
Recency bias helped detect pattern changes in the environment.

In the ancestral environment, these heuristics saved lives.

In trading, they destroy accounts.

Daniel knew this intellectually. He taught it to students. He understood the research.

And it didn't matter.

Because knowing about biases doesn't protect you from them. Intelligence doesn't protect you. Education doesn't protect you.

Systems protect you.

Checklists. Protocols. Mechanical execution. Automated stops. Social isolation. Pre-commitment.

You can't think your way out of cognitive biases. Your thinking IS the bias.
