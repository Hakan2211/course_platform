## Fundamentals and Dilution: Understanding How Companies Change Your Ownership

In the <CustomLink href="01-02-shares-structure">Share Structure & The IPO Process</CustomLink> lesson,
we built our understanding of share structure from the ground up — from authorized shares down to the float.
We visualized it as a "Magic Market Box" with flowing share particles representing the tradable float.

Now we need to address something that can dramatically alter that box overnight:<br/>

**Dilution.**

If you've ever watched a stock you own suddenly drop 15% in pre-market trading after the company announced
a "capital raise," you've experienced dilution firsthand.
It's one of the most important — and often misunderstood — concepts in trading.

Understanding dilution isn't just about protecting yourself from losses.
It's about identifying opportunities. Some of the biggest price moves in small-cap stocks happen around dilutive events,
and if you know what to look for, you can position yourself on the right side of those moves.

## What is Dilution?

Let's start with the core concept.

**Dilution occurs when a company issues new shares, reducing the ownership percentage of existing shareholders.**

Think back to our pizza analogy from the share structure lesson.
You own 1 slice out of 8 total slices — that's 12.5% of the pizza.
Now imagine the company cuts 4 more slices from thin air and sells them to new people.
You still have your 1 slice, but now there are 12 slices total. Your ownership just dropped from 12.5% to 8.3%.

**Your slice didn't get smaller, but your percentage of the whole pie did.**

This is the mathematical reality of dilution. And here's why it matters:
**when ownership is diluted, so is value.** If the company is worth $100 million and
there are 10 million shares, each share represents $10 of value. Add 5 million new shares (increasing to 15 million total),
and suddenly each share only represents $6.67 of value — even if nothing about the business fundamentally changed.

### The Two-Part Impact on Stock Price

Dilution hits a stock price in two ways:

**1\. Mathematical Dilution (Immediate)**

This is the pure math we just described. More shares = each share is worth less.

**2\. Supply Shock (Market Dynamics)**

When millions of new shares suddenly hit the market,
you've created a massive increase in supply. Remember our Magic Market Box? The float just expanded dramatically.
If demand doesn't increase proportionally, the price must fall to find a new equilibrium.

<Alert variant="warning">
  <AlertTitle>The Double Whammy</AlertTitle>
  <AlertDescription>
    A stock can drop 20-40% on a dilutive offering even if the mathematical
    dilution is only 15%. The extra drop comes from the supply shock and
    investor sentiment turning negative.
  </AlertDescription>
</Alert>

## Why Do Companies Dilute?

Before we dive into the mechanics, we need to understand the "why."
Companies don't dilute shareholders for fun — they do it because they need capital.
The reason they need that capital determines whether the dilution is "good" or "bad."

### The Good Reasons (Potentially Accretive)

**Strategic Acquisitions**

A company raises $100 million by issuing new shares to acquire a competitor.
If that competitor generates enough profit to offset the dilution and then some,
the acquisition is accretive — it actually makes each share more valuable despite the increase in share count.

**Example:**<br/>

Company A has 10M shares and generates $10M in annual profit =
$1.00 EPS. They issue 2M new shares (20% dilution) to acquire Company B,
which generates $3M in profit. New total: 12M shares generating $13M profit =
$1.08 EPS. Dilution occurred, but EPS increased.

**Growth Capital with Clear ROI**

A biotech company raises $50 million to fund a Phase 3 clinical trial for a drug
that has already shown promising Phase 2 results.
If the trial succeeds, the potential revenue dwarfs the dilution.

**Strengthening the Balance Sheet**

A company in a cyclical industry raises capital to pay down high-interest debt.
This reduces their cash burn and gives them runway to weather a downturn.

### The Bad Reasons (Value Destructive)

**Burning Cash with No Plan**

A company with negative margins and no path to profitability keeps doing offerings just to stay alive another quarter.
Each offering is a countdown timer to the next one. This is the "death spiral" scenario common in speculative micro-caps.

**Funding a Questionable Acquisition**

The company overpays for an acquisition that doesn't fit their core business,
destroying shareholder value while insiders collect bonuses for "doing a deal."

**Enriching Insiders**

The company issues millions of shares as executive compensation while the stock price languishes.
This is silent, ongoing dilution that compounds year after year.

<Alert variant="info">
  <AlertTitle>The Trader's Question</AlertTitle>
  <AlertDescription>
    When you see a dilutive offering announced, immediately ask: **"What are
    they using the money for?"** Most offerings are destructive in small-cap
    stocks.
  </AlertDescription>
</Alert>

## The Dilution Framework: SEC Filings Decoded

Now let's get tactical. How do companies actually execute dilution? And more importantly, how do you see it coming?<br/>
This is where understanding SEC filings becomes your superpower as a trader.

### The Foundation: Form S-3 (The Shelf Registration)

Think of the **S-3** as pre-approval from the SEC to sell securities over the next three years.

**What it does:**

→ The company files a comprehensive registration statement with the SEC<br/>
→ Once declared "EFFECTIVE," the company can sell securities "off the shelf" at any time<br/>
→ Typically allows registration of $100M-$500M+ in potential offerings<br/>
→ The registration is good for up to 3 years<br/>

**Why it matters to traders:**

The filing of an S-3 creates what's called a "stock overhang." The market knows the company has a loaded gun.
They can dilute at any moment over the next three years, even if they don't have to right now.

**The "EFFECT" Notice:**

After the SEC reviews the S-3, they issue an "EFFECT" notice — this is the green light. The shelf is now active.
From this moment, the company can pull the trigger on an offering with just 1-2 days notice.

<Alert variant="danger">
  <AlertTitle>Critical Point</AlertTitle>
  <AlertDescription>
    An S-3 is NOT an offering. It's the legal prerequisite FOR offerings. The
    actual dilution happens later, when the company "takes down" shares from
    that shelf.
  </AlertDescription>
</Alert>
